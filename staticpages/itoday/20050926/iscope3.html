SEEDHI BAAT ON AAJ TAK

"There is an erosion of leadership in the BJP"

Former BJP leader K.N. Govindacharya spoke to Editor Prabhu Chawla on the politics of the Sangh Parivar.

Q. Do you feel saddened by the developments in the BJP?
A. I have worked for the sake of ideology for the past 40-45 years. Out of that I have worked for a political party for only 12 years. I feel political parties are not reflecting any ideology.

Q. But how can one forget his past?
A. It is not necessary to forget the past but one cannot live in the past.

Q. Aren't you a swayamsevak now?
A. Yes, I am.

Q. The people who run the BJP are mostly from the Sangh.
A. Anyone who goes to a shakha even once is a swayamsevak. I believe I am still a swayamsevak.

Q. Most of the senior BJP leaders are from the Sangh. Are they now acting irresponsibly?
A. The Sangh trains its swayamsevaks at shakhas and expects them to follow the path shown by others. Some swayamsevaks do better than others.

Do you mean there is no difference between your party and other political parties?
A. There doesn't seem to be a big difference in terms of ideology and characteristics. But I do believe that a certain gap has developed between the leaders in power and the ordinary workers.

Q. When you were in the BJP its slogan was "Party with a difference". Now there is no trace of that.
A. There is an erosion of leadership at the district level and above.

Q. Do you mean the present leadership?
A. There is a decline and disconnect from values and issues.

Q. What about the Sangh?
A. The Sangh also needs to think about its ideology, attitude, style of functioning and generation gap.

Q. You had suggested to L.K. Advani that he should wind up the party?
A. I spoke to Advaniji only two times in the past five years that too via telephone. And I don't want to say anything about the conversation.

Q. Have you spoken to him?
A.Yes.

Q. Are you preparing to create a consensus in the country?
A. Yes, I am busy working on a wider social context.
 
Switching to Militant Mode
 
  PICTURE SPEAK
 
Rao (second from right) at a TJS camp  

HYDERABAD The Telangana Rashtra Samithi (TRS) is going in for a makeover. Party chief and Union Labour Minister K. Chandrasekhara Rao has put the TRS in militant mode with the launch of the Telangana Jagarana Samithi (TJS).

Significantly, the TJS is patterned on the RSS. Ale Narendra, TRS second-in-command, has drawn up the blueprint that involves training of 605 TJS preraks who, in turn, will impart training in martial arts to 3,630 UPA preraks. They will train six sainiks in each village and municipal ward in Telangana and all of them, a total of 1,08,000 TJS sainiks, will gather at Hyderabad for a show of strength on October 28.

"TJS cadres will hit back if anti-Telangana forces test their patience," warns Rao. However, he also tried to allay apprehensions about law and order problems, saying the TJS is "a peace-loving army". But worries that this "force of strong activists" may spin out of control persist.

-By Amarnath K. Menon
 
Chaotic Times
 

THIRUVANANTHAPURAM With election time approaching, political parties in Kerala have started entering into alliances of the bizarre kind. Specially the opposition LDF which is finding it hard to explain some of them. Political opponents and those who suffered excesses during the Emergency are lambasting the CPI(M) for its "poll adjustments" with the "Butcher of the Emergency" K. Karunakaran, who left the Congress in May.

The LDF's secular credentials are also being questioned as it allowed the IFDP, the only NDA ally in Kerala, to enter "through the back door" by merging with Kerala Congress (Joseph), a LDF constituent. Meanwhile, the UDF has welcomed back R. Balakrishna Pillai of the Kerala Congress (B). Reason for his return: his attempts to enter the LDF by merging with Janata Dal (U) were thwarted by the CPI(M) and other partners. Watch this space.

-By M.G. Radhakrishnan
Ambitious Aims
 
  PICTURE SPEAK
ON A MISSION: Singh

DELHI The man appointed to steer India's cultural diplomacy is all set to move it from the periphery to the mainstream. For starters, Dr Karan Singh, who took over as president of the Indian Council for Cultural Relations (ICCR) recently, has decided to break precedent and sit at the ICCR office. "I don't want to be a president in absentia," says Singh. This means that mandarins at the cultural body would have to be on their toes. However, Singh has the advantage of leading a new team-Pavan K. Varma is the new director-general and there are two new deputies. "My agenda is to make cultural diplomacy an important element of India's strategy," says Singh.

On the anvil are four more cultural centres of India in Kathmandu, Kabul, Washington and Abu Dhabi in addition to the existing 18. Singh first mooted the idea of a cultural centre at Washington when he was India's ambassador to the US 15 years ago. He is also working on some cultural CBMS to supplement the India-Pakistan peace process. However, his biggest challenge will be cutting through the red tape to give wings to his ambitious agenda.

-By Saurabh Shukla

SANTOS DEMOISELLE WATCH FROM CARTIER; Rs 5,41,000

Flight of Fancy

The Santos Demoiselle is the feminine incarnation of the Santos de Cartier collection. It is named after the featherweight aircraft designed to embody lightness by Alberto Santos-Dumont and is a tribute to the hero of early aviation, who inspired Cartier to design a seductive, streamlined timepiece. The case and bracelet are in 18 carat white gold and the dial is enshrined in a square of diamonds. Bright and delicate, its polished flat links conceal a hidden clasp, and its ergonomic design traces a fluid line around the wrist.
