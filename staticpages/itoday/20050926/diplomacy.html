   DIPLOMACY: INDO-PAK TIES
 
Staying The Course But ...

... the absence of any new announcements after the Manmohan- Musharraf meeting shows there are many roadblocks to overcome
By Anil Padmanabhan in New York
 
When Prime Minister Manmohan Singh and Pakistan President Pervez Musharraf met for dinner at the New York Palace Hotel there was no dearth of bonhomie at the table. Musharraf even offered a culinary suggestion to External Affairs Minister K. Natwar Singh, when he asked him to try the food at Cucoos-a new restaurant in Lahore-during his next visit to Pakistan. It was in sharp contrast to the weighty matters that both leaders had on their agenda.
RENEWING THE PEACE COMMITMENT: Musharraf (left) with Manmohan

It was, therefore, a surprise when Manmohan and Musharraf, after over four hours of deliberations and a five-course continental dinner, issued a bland statement that was read out by the Pakistan President. The last paragraph of the statement reiterated India and Pakistan's commitment to come up with a mutually acceptable resolution of the Jammu and Kashmir problem and "that possible options for a peaceful, negotiated settlement in this regard should continue to be pursued in a sincere spirit and purposeful manner". The language was similar to that used in an announcement in September 2004 and did not have the urgency of the joint statement issued in April this year which emphasised the need to resolve the Kashmir issue.

The collective sigh of the 100-odd Indian and Pakistan mediapersons summed up the response to what was probably left unsaid in the document, especially as the two leaders left without taking any questions. Manmohan's acceptance of Musharraf's invitation to visit Pakistan this year-the prime minister maintained that he would have to check his calendar while accepting the invite-indicates much more had transpired in the meeting than what the joint statement would have everyone believe.

The run-up to the bilateral meeting, which completely overshadowed the annual United Nations General Assembly deliberations, gave equally conflicting signals, with both sides sticking to their widely known stands. While India made it a point to refer to the continued flow of terrorism from PoK, Musharraf stuck to his refrain on Kashmir. He made a passing reference to the disputed UN resolution on Kashmir, saying, "Resolutions of the United Nations, especially the Security Council's decisions, must be implemented." This was not surprising, considering that in his preceding statement he had said, "We cannot ignore the legacy of festering problems left by the past. Peace and justice must come to the peoples of Palestine as well as Jammu and Kashmir."

A day earlier, the Indian side made its point when Manmohan, in his widely publicised meeting with US President George W. Bush, said that "the flow of terrorism" from Pakistan had not stopped. While India has been pushing strongly for clear evidence of reduction of terrorism for any movement forward on Kashmir, the Pakistan delegation wanted some announcement on reduction of Indian troops in the Valley. But with Musharraf publicly mentioning old positions like the Security Council resolution-meant for his domestic audience-it was apparent that the two sides had hit a roadblock and decided not to make any significant announcements in New York.

However, despite the battle lines being so apparently drawn, the actual meeting took place in very cordial circumstances. But for the bland statement, the two sides parted on good terms with the promise that the two leaders would meet again. The fact of the matter is that it is the political leadership which has driven the dialogue and has been the clinching factor in clearing the way for the Srinagar-Muzaffarabad bus service.

Some officials privy to the discussion maintained that "the relationship had come a long way and did not require making pronouncements every time the two leaders met". They added that the measure of success would be the action that both sides would take in the weeks ahead. It may well be so, especially as the leadership of both nations, looking at their domestic constituencies, would not want to ink any deal on contentious issues on foreign soil. From the Indian perspective, the meeting between Musharraf and the Hurriyat leaders on September 17 would further complicate matters.

Meanwhile, Manmohan used the trip to touch base with Bush over the progress of the nuclear pact which seemed to run into turbulence over India's stand on Iran's nuclear waywardness. Manmohan moved fast to clear the air on the contentious issue of possible proliferation by Iran. At the same time, however, India made a case for keeping the door open to negotiations. "Diplomacy must be given a chance to succeed," Manmohan told Bush in their 30-minute meeting. Foreign Secretary Shyam Saran also said that India had clarified its stand on Iran. "When President Bush referred to Iran, our prime minister spelt out India's position on the issue. India has been resolute that Iran should abide by its nuclear obligations under the Nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty," he said.

But it was Manmohan's remark that he was surprised by former prime minister A.B. Vajpayee's criticism of the Indo-US nuclear accord that raised a domestic storm. Former external affairs minister and BJP leader Jaswant Singh said it was "unfortunate" that Manmohan had mentioned it to Bush when convention demanded that domestic politics be kept out of such summit meetings. Even the CPI, a UPA ally, came out against the prime minister. However, Defence Minister Pranab Mukherjee defended the prime minister, saying he had done nothing wrong. It was a controversy that Manmohan could have done well without, given the need for bipartisan support on foreign policy issues. The prime minister isn't likely to hear the end of it when he returns home.
