Mail of the Week
 
  PICTURE SPEAK
"Is He Ready?", September 12

"Rahul Gandhi's capability to live up to people's expectations will continue to be suspect unless his training begins at the grassroots level."

Navneet Dhawan, Delhi

Brand Politics

With the Gandhi surname and diehard supporters in Amethi, Rahul can reach Parliament but he needs to prove his mettle and worth ("Is He Ready?", September 12). He may be another Rajiv Gandhi in the making but he still has a long way to go. He must focus on the development work in his constituency rather than criticise Mulayam Singh Yadav's government.

Bal Govind, Bareilly

Rahul should work as an ordinary MP and show results. Merely criticising the Uttar Pradesh Government for all the ills afflicting the largest state in the country will take him nowhere. Those who want to push the Gandhi scion either into the government or the party should ponder on the desirability of such a move. He has to become more articulate in Parliament and work with the grassroots workers not only in Amethi but also in other districts of Uttar Pradesh.

D.B.N. Murthy, Bangalore

What is so special about Rahul? Is it not true that he has not proved himself an able parliamentarian? Till now, he has not contributed even a bit towards strengthening his party and the Government. Even in Uttar Pradesh, Rahul has failed to emerge as a heavyweight. Also, politics at the national level has altered in a big way since the days of Nehru, Indira and even Rajiv. The expectations of Rahul donning the family mantle in all glory are ridden with apprehensions.

T.S. Pattabhi Raman, Coimbatore

The way the Congress is promoting Rahul clearly shows the slavish mentality of the party, which cannot think beyond the Nehru-Gandhi dynasty. In other words it is a shameful admission by the Congress that it is a big zero without the Nehru-Gandhi family.

A.K. Sharma, Chandigarh

We have had enough of inexperienced young leaders, whose only credential is their family name. The country needs people who are experienced and have their feet firmly on the ground.

B.N. Gururaj, Bangalore

Despite all the similarities in the demeanours of Rahul and Rajiv Gandhi, the changes in Indian polity will pose serious challenges to Rahul in his march to power. He might just make it for lack of competition within the party and because of a weak opposition in the BJP, but the fact remains that he has not been able to prove himself a deserving candidate.

Abhyuadaya Shrivastava, on e-mail

It is expected that Sonia Gandhi will do her best to make her son the prime contender for the post of the prime minister. But is it not the duty of the Congress high command to focus on the grooming of other young politicians like Sachin Pilot?

Saikat Hazra, Uttarpara

It is a five-year graduation course in Parliament for Rahul so that he can lead the country if the Congress gets another term. The slow-pedalling to power would not raise the eyebrows of contenders within the party.

R.G. Mehrotra, Kanpur

 

Give Them Their Due

I was moved to tears reading the stories of the people who were the victims of the anti-Sikh riots ("Justice Denied", September 12). I cannot in the least blame any Sikh for hating Hindus and feeling that India is an uncaring country. I have great respect for people like Harbans Kaur, who has really personified the words of Jesus and Gandhi: "Love your enemy". On behalf of my fellow Hindus, I would like to sincerely apologise and sympathise with the Sikhs. We are not their enemy.

Charu Kulkarni, Bangalore

Thousands of innocent Sikhs were butchered and yet successive governments have failed to nail a single culprit in 21 years. Shamelessness and apathy, not the complicity of the judicial process, are the right explanation for this. When crimes against humanity like this go unpunished, our claims to being a successful democracy ring hollow. We too let the nation down by not reacting sensibly when such barbaric acts take place.

Dhirendra Mishra, Allahabad

The horror of the riots still lingers, though we try to keep blanking it out from our minds. The way many Sikhs bounced back after the 1984 tragedy is a lesson in itself.

D. Manikyala Rao, Gudivada

The anti-Sikh riots were a disgrace to the country. Why is the common man denied justice in India? Is it only limited to the rich and the influential?

Abhinav Vats, Gurgaon

Taxing Trouble

    THE GREAT GAMBLE

If India's record in implementation of pro-poor schemes is any indication, there is little hope that the National Rural Employment Guarantee Scheme will bring deliverance from poverty ("Mother of All Sops", September 12).

H.R. Bapu Satyanarayana, Mysore

The NREG scheme is a novel social project. It has the potential to change the lives of many people in this country.
Jinu Mathew, on e-mail

With most of the money earmarked for the NREG scheme likely to be siphoned off by the unscrupulous, the mother of all sops can turn out to be the mother of all scams.
Rakesh Chatrath, Dehradun

Finance Minister P. Chidambaram and his advocate-wife are wealthy and honest enough not to have illegally taken a CBDT favour ("A Brief Storm", September 12). It is to be noted that Nalini appeared for the CBDT even when her husband was not finance minister. An individual must not lose the right to gain from the government for his/her professional talent just because he/she happens to be related to a minister.

Madhu Agrawal, Delhi

Taxing Trouble

The arrest of journalist Gautam Dhir by the Punjab Police proves how unsafe citizens are in this country ("Arresting Trouble", September 12). Dhir's release without completing the formality of court proceedings was possible only because the media was backing him. The plight of ordinary citizens daring to voice against erring policemen can be imagined.

Subhash C. Agrawal, Delhi

Far From Reality

We are disappointed to find that the story on Bangladesh was subjective and showed a palpable lack of appreciation of the various developments in Bangladesh ("New Hot Spot of Terror", September 5). We would like to mention here that the people and the Government of Bangladesh under Prime Minister Begum Khaleda Zia have reiterated their contempt for violence and extremism. The suggestion that the country harbours terrorist camps also does not correspond to reality. It has been the consistent policy of the Bangladesh Government not to allow its territories to be utilised by forces inimical to our neighbours, including India. The people and the Government of Bangladesh value their relations with India and are keen to further strengthen ties to create energies for the growth, prosperity and stability of our region.

M. Anwarul Haq, minister (press), Bangladesh high commission, Delhi

Correction

The story "King-Size Duel" (August 29) wrongly mentioned that in the 1998 Lok Sabha elections Shubhanginiraje Gaekwad lost the Baroda seat to Satyajit Gaekwad by 17 votes. It was Jitendra Sukhadia who was defeated by 17 votes in the 1996 general elections and Shubhanginiraje stood at the third place. The error is regretted.
