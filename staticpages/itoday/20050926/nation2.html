   NATION: RAILWAYS
 
On The Fast Track
 
Lalu, who calls wagons railway ka kamau ghora (railway's breadwinner), has personally led the campaign for improving freight capacity (see box: Lalu's Lessons). Within a month of taking charge of the Railway Ministry, he conducted a surprise inspection of a goods train at Danapur near Patna, which triggered a nationwide inspection of goods train. Most trains were found to be overloaded beyond the permitted weight. The potential loss to railways on the 168 trains inspected that day was close to Rs 3.5 lakh. This triggered the decision to increase wagon capacity.
"Mr Yadav made decision making easier with his quick seal of approval."
R.K. SINGH,FORMER CHAIRMAN, RLY BOARD

Another bane of the railways' freight business has been limited hours of loading and unloading, which holds up wagons for hours and days. Lalu provided a solution by asking the Railway Board a simple question: how many hours do our trains run? The answer came: 24 hours. So, for how many hours do we load trains? The answer: eight hours in the daytime. Lalu's repartee was, if trains can run 24 hours, why can't they be loaded 24 hours. This set off a massive drive in increasing hours and improving facilities for loading of trains.

LALU'S LESSONS ...
Lalu is known for his entertaining, even if meaningless, one liners. But as railways minister, he fits into the role of a demanding CEO
"Board should chalk out a time-bound action plan for completion of ongoing works and complete maximum number of works by the end of this year. I should be apprised of the progress . . . every quarter." -June 2005 letter asking the Railway Board to speed up investment in operational efficiency

"I request you to look into this matter personally and ensure that all tender notices concerning parcel and sundry earnings contracts are issued within a period of 15 days." -July 2005 letter to GMs asking for time-bound work

"We should set a target of 700 m tonne freight loading in 2005-6. It's a challenging task which would call for excellent teamwork and rigorous implementation of the action plan." -May 5, 2005 letter setting out 'Mission 700 MT' for freight loading.

"We should benchmark our train examination practices with the best in the world and make suitable investments in upgrading infrastructure facilities." -March 18, 2005 letter asking for higher running time for wagons

The efforts are showing in both profits and praises (see box on customers' views). After crossing the 600 million tonnes target for annual freight last year, Lalu has spelt out an action plan for achieving the 700 million tonne mark this year and is following up regularly on the progress.

On the passenger side of the business too, a similar drive is on to get more bang out of the same buck. The Indian Railways loses money on second-class passenger tickets. The attempt is to carry more passengers, without adding more coaches. Officials down to the level of divisional railway managers (DRM) have been empowered to take coaches off trains running with vacant seats and have them attached to trains with waiting lists. Such re-balancing was earlier done very selectively by officials senior than DRMs. Steps are also being taken to keep wait-listed passengers from cancelling tickets by offering them options ranging from upgrades to change in date, class and destination (see Passenger Privileges).

But nothing beats the innovation and boldness of the measures taken to overhaul the perpetually sick-though conceptually sound-business of catering and parcel. The rewards can only be called a windfall (see box: No More Free Lunches). This is when the minister has been decidedly supportive of non-populist measures.

Even when Lalu has been populist it has gone in the railways' favour. One of the conditions Lalu set very early for his officers was a freeze on hike in passenger fares. In fact, he reduced the second-class non-reserved ticket fares by Re 1, which cost the railways Rs 250 crore. But, in general, freezing fares makes perfect business sense at a time when competition (road transport on one hand and low-cost airlines on the other) is breathing down the Indian Railways' neck. Though passenger demand isn't a worry on the aggregate level-in 2004-5 the network carried 550 crore passengers, or 1.5 crore passengers a day-the threat is of losing the creamy layer of AC class passengers.

... AND HIS WISDOM
"Railways is like a Jersey cow. Milk it well, or it will get sick." (He means: The railways' revenue potential is huge but we aren't using it optimally. That's why it's not earning enough)

"The wagon is the bread-earning horse. Load it well, don't let it rot in the yards." (He means: Freight is where the railways gets most of its income from. Increase the usage time of wagons)

The hike in diesel prices announced on September 6 will raise the railways' fuel cost by at least Rs 250 crore. But far from complaining, the railways are celebrating. The board has not only decided not to raise fares and freight rates but also proposed discounts for customers who can promise to fill in wagons on return trips. Says Sudhir Kumar, officer on special duty with the minister: "The diesel hike is a blessing in disguise. It gives me an opportunity to wean customers away from road transport to the railways if I keep my rates frozen by playing on volumes." Since the 1950s, the railways' share in freight transport has plummeted from 89 per cent to 33 per cent. But in the past one year, its share in coal and steel transport has increased.

Kumar, a joint secretary from the Bihar cadre, is Lalu's Man Friday in the ministry. Known for his integrity and hard work, he has understood the Indian Railways inside out and blends well with the Railway Board. He influences, if not determines, the priorities, but ensures that the board is never overruled in launching bold new initiatives. R.K. Singh, who worked as Railway Board chairman under both Nitish Kumar and Lalu, found decision making faster under Lalu. Though he praises Kumar for his very hands on style, he admits that Lalu's bigger clout with the UPA Government is an advantage to the board in pushing through decision with other ministries.

CASH AND CARRY: Freight income has risen by Rs 2,800 cr in two years

Buoyed by the turnaround, Lalu is aiming to match the Indian Railways' profit match Reliance Industries'. His officials see a real probability of net revenues (profit before payment of dividend to government) in 2005-6 matching at least the 2004-5 net profits of Reliance Industries-Rs 7,500 crore. Kumar would like to call the railways' profit projection an Infosys kind of market guidance.

Even if such comparison are purely academic, they have a strong positive underlying message-that Lalu and his men want to benchmark the railways' performance with that of the Indian private sector. Such benchmarking will hopefully infuse even more commercial principles in the functioning of the railways.

The railways, of course, has a long journey to cover before it attains the efficiency levels of the private sector. The improvements made so far, though significant, are incremental. It has to take giant leaps in improving its physical performance, without which the financial turnaround could peter out. One such leap would be early implementation of dedicated freight corridor-tracks exclusively made for superfast freight trains running at speeds of 150 kmph as opposed to the average 25 kmph at present. Globally, container cargo's share in freight is rising. Railways will need to invest in container traffic. The question is, should the railways alone be trusted to take such giant steps, or should the Government set up a parallel body to implement and run projects like freight corridors-an option the Planning Commission may prefer. There is also the issue of turning the railways into a corporation, so that it ceases to be a department.

CUSTOMER VIEW
"Lalu has no scheme of his own. He's only carrying forward the work of Nitish Kumar." SUSHIL KUMAR MODI, PRESIDENT, BIHAR BJP

"The railways have become more friendly and are reaching out to customers. MANOJ GAUR, MANAGING DIRECTOR, JAIPRAKASH ASSOCIATES

Irrespective of what the future holds, going by the performance so far, the question being asked is not whether Lalu has been a good minister, but why? Why, his critics justifiably ask, has a person who was at the helm in Bihar during its worst years, suddenly become a good administrator? Why did he save this administrative acumen for the railways and not display it in his home state? One explanation is Lalu's possible desire to prove his administrative skills in a ministry his bete noires Ram Vilas Pawan and Nitish Kumar have headed in the past. Another explanation is that Lalu may have surprised himself with what he achieved in the railways and having done that, wants to build on it.

"Lalu has no scheme of his own. He's only carrying forward the work of Nitish Kumar."
SUSHIL KUMAR MODI, PRESIDENT, BIHAR BJP

But a more probable explanation is sheer chance. A combination of circumstances, desire and compulsions has delivered to him what nobody ever expected Lalu to deliver. A more relevant question is: what happens after the Bihar polls? Will Lalu prefer Bihar to the railways? Almost certainly yes. Will railways continue to be on the growth curve after Lalu goes? There is no reason why it can't. Will Lalu apply the lessons in good governance learnt at Rail Bhavan to Bihar if he gets the chance? That will be Lalu's real test.

-with Satarupa Bhattacharjya and Puja Mehra
