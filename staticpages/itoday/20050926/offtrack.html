   OFFTRACK: BHOPAL
 
The Paper Trail

A museum of rare and an old newspapers and periodicals chronicles the growth of Indian journalism
By Ambreesh Mishra
 
  PICTURE SPEAK
DOCUMENTING HISTORY: Old newspaper at the Madhavrao Sapre Museum

In today's world, newspapers and magazines are the chroniclers of history. But shouldn't somebody document the history of these chroniclers? This was the thought that led Vijaydutt Sridhar, former editor of the Hindi daily Nava Bharat, to set up the Madhavrao Sapre Museum of Newspapers in Bhopal. Started in 1984, the storehouse of yellow, crumbling pages of newspapers and periodicals stands testimony to the growth of Indian journalism.

Sridhar first developed an interest in the topic when he was approached by the Sagar and Jabalpur universities in the 1980s to prepare the course material on the history of journalism in central India. Hampered by the lack of reference material Sridhar realised the value of a proper archive. Thus began the museum's journey during which it moved from the Rani Kamlapati Mahal to its current quarters.

Starting with pre-1947 publications like the Harijan, and Bal Gangadhar Tilak's Kesari, the museum traces the course of India's freedom struggle. When it was last catalogued in December 2004, it had over 11,000 copies of newspapers and magazines including gems like Hicky's Bengal Gazette, the first newspaper of modern India, Madan Mohan Malviya's Hindi weekly Abhyudaya and Maulana Abul Kalam Azad's Al-Hilal. Besides original copies of Hindi, Urdu, Gujarati, Sanskrit and Marathi newspapers, it also houses international publications like Nature, Punch, and the Illustrated London News. Started with Sridhar's archive, the collection was built up through contributions from individual collectors. "Without their donations the initiative would have failed," says Sridhar.

The museum is divided into three sections: two for publications dating from 1681 to 1920 and from 1921 to 2005 and a third for files and clippings. Great attention is paid to preservation and photocopying is not allowed. The collection has proved a boon for researchers and so far more than 500 scholars have availed of it for their MPhil and PhD research. To extend its reach, the museum launched its website, www.mpsapresangrahalaya.org, in May this year.

It has gone beyond being a repository of old publications. In 2001 it brought out the first volume of its two-part project, Samagra Bharatiya Patrakarita, on the history of Indian journalism from 1780 to 1900. The next volume will cover 1901-1950 and include newspapers and magazines published in areas now in Pakistan and Bangladesh. This will add a new chapter to its already extensive collection.
