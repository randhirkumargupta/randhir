   GUEST COLUMN: Javagal Srinath
 

Bowlers' Blues

India must identify its World Cup pace quartet and get them a specialist bowling coach

 
 
As India played Australia in the Champions Trophy, I thought about how the event was an opportunity for our bowlers. The balance in cricket is now heavily skewed in favour of bat over ball. But batsmen have struggled on decent wickets in the Champions Trophy and it was a chance for our young bowlers to establish themselves the way bowlers from other teams had. India's bowlers should have been in a situation to win matches but were unable to do so. Is there time to rectify the situation? For starters, I think India should finalise its four bowlers for the World Cup right now. Then, win or lose, play those four bowlers in as many games as they can. If we are not sure of our Cup quartet today, then things do not look good.

Every department of a squad is, in fact, a sub-team. Within the bowling unit, spinners and fast bowlers form different teams. The proper functioning of a fast bowlers' 'team' depends on their collective strength, variation (not just in every individual bowler but the group) and experience.

PERPLEXING: The team thinktank has not paid full attention to Irfan's natural ability
Experience is the only constant in a bowling team. Without it, the 'team' operates like a group of players missing vital parts. In a good bowling 'team', the experienced bowlers help transfer knowledge to the new entrants. This systematic inheritance of knowledge by the rookies may not always be possible because players go in and out of the squad.

An alternative means to possess this experience is to hire a bowling coach. Without experienced bowlers or a knowledgeable bowling coach, the bowling 'team' is disoriented and orphaned. Champion sides like Australia and professional units like England, South Africa and Pakistan have realised the absolute necessity of a bowling coach.

The reason why this is happening is very obvious: the new rules and the new formats, the downsizing of the game into shorter versions, has adversely affected the balance between bat and ball. The effects of these changes are now reflected in the Indian team. Rahul Dravid and Greg Chappell have used 10 fast bowlers so far. Out of these, the most experienced duo-Zaheer Khan and Ashish Nehra-were not in favour with management and selectors. Ajit Agarkar was called in mostly for the one-dayers. This meant that the bowlers playing both forms of the game happened to be the most inexperienced. Being a batsman, Rahul will typically be more worried and more focused about his team's batting failures. It is up to the coach to upgrade the bowling. Coach Chappell and sub-coach Ian Frazer (if he is one) have made fairly predictable attempts. These have involved chopping and changing, most under the cover of experimenting and creating a bowlers' pool. Bleeding seniors in the name of building youngsters is nothing but an arbitrary stance.

For the longest time, Zaheer's non-inclusion after being the highest wicket-taker in the English county circuit was baffling. Nehra, lurking anxiously after travelling all over the world to get fit, does not have a clue as to where he belongs. Agarkar's form, after seeing a good gain, appears to have flattened again. Most worryingly, the career of Irfan Pathan has nose-dived and he too finds himself in and out of the side. It was never going to be easy for Irfan after his first round in international cricket. Opposition teams have assessed Irfan's every bowling movement trying to render it predictable. He needs to make rapid changes to his bowling: it could be an extra yard of pace or incorporating new seam movement. Today, Irfan has two choices: to become his own coach as that's the most natural way, or to find a specialist coach who can constantly monitor his progress one-on-one, using technology to track improvement. The most dangerous solution is to ask a distressed Irfan to speak to different bowlers as and when he bumps into them and to then try to follow their ideologies. This resultant bits-and-pieces exercise has killed the player's imagination, an important factor in self-correction and development. Irfan's case is a collective failure of the people concerned who have paid no attention to his natural ability. Upscaling the performances of the younger trio of Munaf Patel, S. Sreesanth and Rudra Pratap Singh must be a priority now. The only solution I can see is to find a suitable bowling coach. The names that crop up are Venkatesh Prasad and T.A. Sekhar, who have worked hands-on with bowlers. One has international exposure, the other pure coaching experience.

India's next destination South Africa is the ideal place for fast bowlers. If India is to build up its bowling 'team' again, the seniormost of its bowlers, Zaheer, needs to be made more responsible and answerable-not just for his own bowling but for the rest of the pack too.

Javagal Srinath took 551 wickets for India between 1991 and 2003
