Chinese Checkers
 
  PICTURE SPEAK
STRATEGIC FRIENDSHIP: Manmohan (left) and Jintao
DELHI Chinese President Hu Jintao's scheduled visit to India in two weeks is already creating ripples in the establishment. After 40 years, the Nathu La Pass was opened to border trade this July, an important landmark in the complicated relations between the two countries. Hu's arrival in Delhi late on November 19 is being viewed as a significant event in the Indo-China Friendship Year.

Information and Broadcasting Minister Priya Ranjan Dasmunshi has informed CPI General Secretary A.B. Bardhan that the UPA Government intends to invite Hu to address the Parliament. For the Chinese President's convenience, the winter session would be advanced to November 22 from November 27, Dasmunshi supposedly told the CPI leader. Later, he denied having said anything as a "UPA Government representative."

When US President George Bush had visited India this March, sections within the Congress party and the Government had wanted him to address the Parliament; but the Left's anti-Bush protestations had made the Congress insecure, sources said. With a world leader again on Indian shores, the idea of a guest address in Parliament has resurfaced. But Washington isn't amused this time. With direct access to the Communist Party of China (CPC), the Indian communists are engaged in parallel diplomacy.

A CPI(M) delegation led by Politburo member Sitaram Yechury recently met CPC politburo members in Beijing. Detailed discussions on Indo-China relations were held with Chinese Foreign Minister Lizhao Xing. The CPI(M) leaders visited China's biggest special economic zone in Hainan province to study the sale of public land.

The Chinese seemed to have told Yechury that the UPA Government shouldn't insist on border talks during Hu's visit. CPC members are understood to have said that it was in India's interest to focus on bilateral trade instead.

China is India's second-largest trade partner after the US. In 2005, $18.7 billion (Rs 84,060 crore) was the value of bilateral trade, up from $1.16 billion (Rs 5,200 crore) in 1995.

By Satarupa Bhattacharjya
 
Saffron Dynasty
 
  PICTURE SPEAK
NEW HEIRESS: (L-R) Rahul, Rao and Munde
MUMBAI Following her late father and BJP leader Pramod Mahajan's footsteps, Poonam Rao formally joined the BJP on October 30, which marked her father's birth anniversary. "I am joining as an ordinary member. I'd like to fulfil my father's dream," she said. Although Poonam has not been given a specific portfolio, she'll be concentrating on women issues and campaign during the forthcoming municipal elections.

Poonam supersedes her brother Rahul, who was touted to be his father's likely heir. But the BJP has barred his formal entry into the party following the drug controversy in June 2006. Rahul said he was "happy" for his sister. "Joining the party is a mere formality. I wish to continue working for the party," he said.

Meanwhile, Poonam has support from uncle Gopinath Munde. But certain sections of the BJP workforce are unhappy with the recent developments. The BJP hitherto was known to be an ardent critic of the Congress due to dynastic politics.

-By Prerana Thakurdesai
 
Divided They Rule
 
BANGALORE When Karnataka's ruling coalition, comprising the BJP and the JD(S), took office in February 2006, it was on the understanding that the office of chief minister will be rotated between the two parties. The pact is now under strain.

Some flashpoints that are driving a deep wedge between them are the delay over Cabinet expansion and nominations to boards and corporations. Chief Minister H.D. Kumaraswamy says he is ready to expand the Cabinet within two hours of the BJP furnishing a list of its nominees. The BJP is miffed that Kumaraswamy did not consider its nominees for the Karnataka Ekkikarana (unification) and Rajyotsava awards.

The parties are going their separate ways in the forthcoming byelection from Chamundeshwari assembly constituency. With the coalition partners clashing over everything, it remains to be seen if the rotation policy will stay.

-By Stephen David
