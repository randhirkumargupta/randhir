   OFFTRACK: JAMSHEDPUR
 

From Mops to Machines

Tata Steel is training unskilled women workers at its plant to take up heavy mechanical work

By Puja Mehra
 
  PICTURE SPEAK

MADE OF STEEL: A member of Tejaswini at the Tata plant
It all appears routine when one looks at the various heavy-work sections at the Tata Steel plant in Jamshedpur with workers in their full-sleeve sky blue shirts, navy-blue pants, hardy boots and yellow helmets going about their chores industriously. However, a close look at the welders cutting metal with thin, sharp blasts of flames or the drivers behind the huge steering wheels of giant dumpers and earth-movers, brings home the difference. Some of the workers have vermilion on their foreheads. These workers, called Tejaswinis at Tata Steel, are women who rub shoulders with men as operators of cranes and mechanics of heavy engineering equipment.

Till late, most of these women, being unskilled, either swept, cleaned or did odd jobs at the plant. However, in 2002, Tata Steel launched Tejaswini, a programme to train unskilled women workers so that they could take up heavy mechanical work. In the 98-year history of the steel major, which may soon become the world's fifth largest steel manufacturer, it was a new beginning of sorts.

And so it was for Usha Ben, a sweeper at the plant. "I was ashamed of the job I did earlier," she says. She can now open the engine of a dumper, grease it, replace parts such as crank shafts, pistons and bearings and get it back on the road. Similarly, Jusmi Judu, a labourer till yesterday is now a mechanic at the coke plant. "Apart from my job of looking after the lubrication and maintenance of machinery, if there is a shortage of personnel for manning the chute or welding, I am immediately called upon," says Judu. The programme has already trained many batches and now over 30 Tejaswinis work at the plant. Says Managing Director B. Muthuraman, "The Tejaswini programme signals the end of male domination in jobs, at least in the workers category."

Tejaswini has dented the mindset that heavy work-load can only be handled by men. A survey of 149 machinery and equipment, chemicals, motor vehicles, food products and beverages and construction companies done by the Confederation of Indian Industries about 12 months ago to sensitise the industry on gender inequalities found that only 6 per cent of the employees at these organisations were women. Tejaswini, hopefully, will be the right example to follow to change that.
