It's A Dream Run

It is raining dollars for India's top companies as they bite into the $300-billion global pie of information technology services

 
 
THE FAST AND THE FURIOUS
Earnings are soaring at a break-neck speed for Indian IT companies as they grab more and more global customers.
TCS :
Added 11,720 employees this financial year to take the total staff strength to 78,200.

Won 58 clients since April to take the total to 742, in which 7 Fortune 10 companies figure.

Infosys :
Already recruited 13,435 employees this financial year to take the headcount to 66,150.

Added 83 clients this fiscal. Of the total 476 clients, 93 give it annual business of $50m each.

Wipro :
Hired 7,437 employees this year to take the total to 61,179. Plans to poach laterally.

Acquired 113 new clients since April. Of 593 customers, 35 are $20m-plus annual accounts.
Infosys Technologies clocked an annual income of $1 billion in April 2004-23 years after it was born. The next $1-billion milestone was reached 23 months later. Infosys has projected that it will take less than 12 months to cross the $3-billion mark in March 2007. India's star it company isn't the only one that is performing spectacularly. Profits of the three torchbearers of the sector-Tata Consultancy Services (TCS), Infosys and Wipro-have risen at an average of 31 per cent since 2000-01 beating the 7 per cent growth in global it spending. The website for Infosys quotes its chief mentor N.R. Narayana Murthy: "The raison d'être of every corporate body is to ensure predictability, sustainability and profitability of revenues year after year." Indian it exports grew 28.5 per cent to $36 billion last year or 10 per cent of the global market.

As governments, companies and organisations are becoming increasingly technology-driven, they are rapidly switching from inhouse teams and legacy service providers such as Accenture, EDs and IBM to the Indian trio. "Though the bulk of our competition still comes from the top global companies, we have been running into TCS and Satyam in certain types of projects," says Kris Wadia, Partner, Global Delivery Network, Accenture. That's because the Indian it services model delivers faster, better and is cheaper, says Wipro's chief financial officer Suresh Senapaty. Azim Premji's Wipro sends only one-third the number of employees to its projects that any of the world leaders normally would. It thus provides the same services with two-thirds employees working on the project from India. As charges are billed at the rate of $11,000 a month per employee stationed onsite, and $4,500 a month for those off-shore, the trio has added clients at the rate of three every two days this fiscal. They now have 749 clients-including some Fortune 500 companies-each of which gives it more than $1 million income a year. Though contracts are being won fast and the rates charged are also steadily rising-Infosys builds in 2-3 per cent rate hikes into new contracts-more than 95 per cent of the revenues come from repeat business where the same customer hands over more of its it services needs to them.

To cater to the exponential demand, they are recruiting at a scorching pace. TCS plans to jack up its headcount by 30,000 employees this year, which is perhaps more than the net additions to the Chinese army, says a TCS spokesperson. Infosys is investing $100 million in training this year and has applied for 3,500 visas to cater to its onsite needs. "We are able to create market and mind share amongst clients and to complement that on the delivery side, we build requisite scale and execution capabilities," said Infosys CEO Nandan Nilekani at its last quarter results.

The cutting edge work that the Indian it industry is executing includes figuring out the optimal performance of Formula One cars for Ferrari in Italy (TCS) and HCL's mid-air anti-aircraft collision software for the world's largest plane manufacturer Boeing.

But the dream run needs to be seen in perspective. India's it exports were a fraction of the total global it spend of $1.5 trillion. Qualitatively too, they have just about reached the mid-level in the chain of it services that begins at the maintenance of it systems and software (procured by clients from the world leaders) and goes up to selling patented products such as Microsoft's Windows. Even the sky will not be the limit when the Indian it champs get there. As Nilekani said: "Everything is up for grabs."


-By Puja Mehra
