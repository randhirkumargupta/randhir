   NATION: RELIGION CENSUS
 
Numbers Game

The first detailed census of Indian citizens based on their faith gives invaluable insights into the religious fabric of the country. But political parties are using the findings for vested interests.
By Malini Goyal
 

Religion and passion often go together. But that a socio-economic survey based on religion would ignite a fierce debate of India's changing religious profile was not expected. At least not on the narrow-focused data on the rising number of Muslims in India. Since the census is all about facts, here are some to put in perspective the debate on India's changing demographics.

Every year in the 1990s, India added 18 million Hindus and five million Muslims to its population.

Muslims were the fastest growing community in India, not just in the 1990s but in the preceding three decades, barring the 1970s (1981 census) when Assam was excluded from the survey. In 1991, Jammu and Kashmir was excluded.

Muslims have the highest fertility rate. But barring the sex ratio, they are at the bottom on virtually every parameter-overall literacy, female literacy and employment.

Amidst the politically charged environment, the message is completely lost that the population growth rate is defined more by socio-economic status rather than religion. Economically, Muslims are the most backward community with the lowest employment rate. Their population is rising faster in poor states like Bihar, Jharkhand, Rajasthan, Uttar Pradesh than in the developed states of Tamil Nadu, Kerala, Andhra Pradesh and Karnataka. Growth rates across all communities in prosperous states like Tamil Nadu, Pondicherry and Maharashtra are much below their national average while growth rates in Uttar Pradesh and Rajasthan are substantially higher than the national average across all communities. Says Jayant Kumar Banthia, census commissioner: "Beyond growth rates, the religion census sends out a larger message to policymakers on overall developmental needs."

The census data also reveals that population growth is more a factor of education and literacy. For instance, barring a few blips, Bihar has among the highest fertility rate, the lowest overall literacy rate and one of the poorest employment rate across all communities. In contrast, Kerala has among the highest literacy levels including women, the lowest fertility rate and among the best sex ratio across all communities. Little wonder then that Bihar's population grew at a faster clip than that of Kerala. Jains have the highest literacy levels and the lowest fertility rate in the country. Ditto for Christians-who rank second on literacy, and very high female literacy and have among the lowest fertility rates.

If the Census 2001 was a harbinger of controversy, it also brought glad tidings. The growth rate of the Hindu population, climbing since 1981, slowed down between 1991 and 2001. Since Hindus constitute the largest chunk of India's population, this is good news for the second most populous nation in the world. Incidentally, Sikhs have the lowest population growth rate in the country.

The differential growth rates of the various communities have changed the demographic structure of the Indian population in the past decade. Between 1991 and 2001, while the population of Hindus shrunk marginally in percentage terms, that of Christians, Sikhs, Jains and Buddhists stagnated. The Muslim community, of course, have grown the fastest, creating a flutter in political circles.

This is the first time that the government undertook such a detailed study of the socio-economic status of various religious communities. Post-independence, the once-in-a-decade census looked at religion data but only by sex and residence and provided limited insights. In many ways, Census 2001 is different from all previous surveys. Besides religion, it was also the first time when the census profiled the Indian population by the assets and household amenities owned by them, the results for which were released last year. The survey is impressive in its size, scale and rigour. Conducted by an army of two million drafted by the Census of India, it had the nose-count man knocking at the doors of every single Indian household across 593 districts in 35 states and Union territories of India. He sought information on age, sex, literacy, work status, language, religion, migration, fertility history, home, assets and household goods.

The census data shatters several myths. Contrary to popular perception, while Jammu and Kashmir, Assam and West Bengal may have the highest percentage of Muslim population, it is in Uttar Pradesh, Maharashtra and Bihar where the largest number of Muslims reside. Curiously, Uttar Pradesh has the largest number of both Hindus and Muslims but both communities have lived in relative harmony in the state. Kerala, Tamil Nadu and Nagaland have the largest number of Christians while Sikhs are concentrated in Punjab, Haryana and Rajasthan.

Religion and region apart, what comes across as a uniform fact is the discrimination against women across all communities. On virtually every parameter-literacy, sex ratio and employment-women trail the community's overall average without exception. Female literacy among Muslims is an abysmal 50.1 per cent (as against the community's overall average of 59.1 per cent) and among Hindus is 53.2 per cent as against the overall average of 65.1 per cent.

Orissa, Himachal Pradesh and Chhattisgarh have the highest percentage of Hindus.

The highest percentage of Muslims is in J&K, Assam and West Bengal.

Nagaland, Meghalaya and Mizoram have highest percentage of Christians.

The highest percentage of Sikhs are in Punjab, Haryana and Chandigarh.
Jains are concentrated in Delhi, Maharashtra and Rajasthan and Buddhists in Sikkim, Arunachal Pradesh and Mizoram.

Believe it or not, this bias against the girl child-represented by the sex ratio (number of females per 1,000 males) in the age group 0-6 years-has become more pronounced after liberalisation virtually across all communities, led by the Sikhs and Jains. It is particularly evident in urban areas. Muslim and Jains are among the most conservative communities when it comes to allowing their womenfolk to work and this insularity of women only gets worse as one moves from rural to urban India. Christians and Jains, comprising barely 3 per cent of the total Indian population, are the most literate communities with women literacy at over 75 per cent.

Surprisingly, women in rural India are better off on most parameters, barring literacy and fertility rate, than women in urban areas. For example, in urban areas the sex ratio is far lower than in rural areas across communities. Overall, sex ratio of 0-6 years population in rural India is 934 while in urban areas it is lower at 906. This contrast is most stark among Hindus-in rural India it is 932, in urban areas it drops to 898.

Also, irrespective of the economic progress, northern India clearly has a bias against the girl child vis-à-vis the South. Sikhs across the country, whether in urban or rural areas, have a strong bias against the girl child-the community has a sex ratio of only 893. This bias seems to have intensified during the 1990s because the sex ratio in the 0-6 years age group has come down to 786. Here again, the bias is not entirely religion specific. Though it is dominated by Sikhs, Punjab has a low sex ratio across all communities. For Hindus, it stands at 846 and for Muslims at 793.

Women also face discrimination when it comes to work in urban areas. Their employment levels in rural India across all communities is far higher than in urban areas. But this may be more of a financial compulsion than a concerted effort to liberate women. For example, 32.6 per cent of rural Hindu women are employed as against 12.5 per cent of urban Hindus. For Muslims, it stands at 17.5 per cent as against 7.7 per cent in urban areas. And for Christians, it stands at 32.6 as against urban India's 21.2 per cent.

The survey also reinforces the thesis that female literacy has a direct impact on the fertility rate, irrespective of the religion followed. Despite being a small community of 42 lakh, Jains are a good role model. The only community with over 90 per cent female literacy, their fertility rate is the lowest in the country. In fact they have emerged as the socio-economically most developed religious group in the country. Some of the states with relatively high (over 70 per cent) female literacy for both Hindus and Muslims are Tamil Nadu, Chhattisgarh, Lakshadweep, Kerala, Delhi, Chandigarh, Mizoram, Pondicherry, Goa and Andaman & Nicobar Islands. Most of these states also show more controlled fertility rates underlining the importance of female literacy in population control.

The census data provides many insights into the country's demographics. If mined well, it could be extremely useful for policy formulation. Instead, the census findings have been reduced to a political football. Already, the decline in the growth rate of the Hindu population vis-à-vis Muslims is poised to become a political tool for the Sangh Parivar. Should that happen, the effort involved and the wealth of information revealed about various communities will be lost to politics.
