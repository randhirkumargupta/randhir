Doctor's Prescription

A gynaecologist by profession, the Rajasthan health minister says he is determined to change the state's pathetic healthcare system. Just how far has he gone, asks India Today's Rohit Parihar.
 

It was an unusual sight at the office of Rajasthan Health Minister Digambar Singh. A woman had traversed 200 km from Bharatpur and had landed in the chamber after working her way through the secretariat security. "She insisted that I examine her," recalls Singh who after much effort, managed to convince her that he was not equipped to do the needful but that he could arrange for a government doctor to see her.

For a man who claims to be a minister most suitable for the job, the visit should not have come as a surprise. A gynecologist by profession, Singh has made it known on more than one occasion that he would use his vast experience to personally improve the state's healthcare system, considered one of the worst in the country.

The state, he admits , has very high infant and maternal mortality rates. "The situation is worse in rural areas," he adds. But even the urban set up ranks far below compared to the national standard. The fill the void, Chief Minister Vasundara Raje has ordered the opening of 461 health sub-centres in desert areas and doubled the number of health workers in the tribal belt.

In what is a move to tackle individual problems, Singh had decided to pay Rs 200 to each doctor, nurse or person who brings to light a case of home delivery, an issue that has dogged the state for long. Currently, Rajasthan records 70 per cent home deliveries. The minister says there must be greater interaction between couples and health staff so as to ensure safe deliveries. "We intend using health workers in listing eligible couples and motivate them to adopt simple and economical family planning measures," continues G.S. Sandhu, secretary, health.

For once, the Government admits there are basic flaws in the measures being implemented to control population. "Most of the time, couples who go in for family planning operations are those who already have big families. That defeats the very purpose of limiting population through such measures," says the health minister.

Doctors, in general, are also being made more accountable. They now have to report for duty both in morning and evening. Their needs too are being taken note of with Singh admitting that doctors in rural areas are not provided adequate facilities. These faciltiies include good residential complexes besides a substantial rural allowance. Right now, prime postings are urban-centric. The effort is to reverse the trend and make village postings more attractive. By involving private sector, the government intends having CT scan machines in 10 districts besides arranging for anesthetists and blood storage facilities at hospitals. As many as 50 centres with blood bank facilities will also be established.

Although Rajasthan is rated as a low-risk state for AIDS, 1,200 fully blown cases have been reported. The Government has been trying to ensure that medicines for them are available at subsidised costs. In yet another innovation, condom machines have been put up in Jaipur in an attempt to target the young which is reluctant to buy these from shops.

The new dispensation wants to generate a system that allows for genuine feedback on changes being brought in. One particular area of concern is the move to make medical education a part of technical and higher education, distinct from the health ministry. This has for the first time helped put the focus on research. Since hospitals attached with medical colleges remain with the health ministry, there have been some administrative problems which need to be sorted out. Corruption too is deeply entrenched and would have to be tackled effectively.

"I will work and fight hard to ensure that the basic faults are corrected," says a determined Singh. But the proof of the words will lie in his actions in the coming days.
