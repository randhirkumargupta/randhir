   OFFTRACK: PRATAPGARH
 
Secret Designs

A family in Rajasthan keeps alive the unique art of Thewa on which it has had a monopoly for centuries
By Rohit Parihar
 

Very curious and prettily wrought." This is how the British described these items that looked like gems encased in gold in the Rajputana Gazetteer in the early 20th century. History and geography have changed since but the sentiment still holds good. Thewa, the unqiue art that created these baubles, continues to appeal to aesthetes-and to the family that has a virtual monopoly on its making.

The preserve of the Sonis of Pratapgarh in Rajasthan, Thewa survives as a secret art almost 200 years after the maharaja of Pratapgarh initiated one of the ancestors of the Sonis into the business in the 19th century. As a rule, no labourer has been hired from outside the family. It is believed that for decades the Sonis did not even let their daughters and sons-in-law into their workshops for fear of the secret being leaked out.

  PICTURE SPEAK
TINSEL TRADE: Thewa artist Soni at work with his granddaughter

What makes Thewa so coveted? The design and the intricacy of the work perhaps. The range of Thewa jewellery and accessories involve the skilful and painstaking use of gems, enamelled gold and foil, silver frames and bubble-free coloured glass. Ram Niwas Soni, at 75 one of the oldest surviving Thewa makers, is willing to elaborate but discreetly.

The sketch of the _design is precisely etched on the gold foil. A set of 15 mini tools is used to chisel, cut or scrape surfaces. The exact process remains undivulged, but broadly speaking, lacquer is filled into a silver frame and the gold foil with the sketch is affixed over it. Ultimately, the bubble-free glass takes the place of lacquer just as a plating of gold replaces the silver surface of the frame. A single product could take around three days to complete. The cost of, say, a set of buttons for a sherwani or a simple necklace could be up to Rs 4,000.

Apart from personal use, Thewa makes for a fitting gift, a tradition set into motion by the erstwhile maharajas who would present visiting Britishers with trinket boxes or perfume bottles. One such item was presented to Queen Victoria by Maharaja Dulput Singh.

The Sonis have bagged six national awards for excellent in craftsmanship. The Indian Posts and Telegraphs even issued a special postage stamp on Thewa. It is a stamp symbolic of not just the unmatched beauty of the art but also its arcane appeal.
